I was 13, and I was afraid of computers.

Behind every new Linux user is a user who wants to make more of their computing device. For most users, that's because they want to breathe new life in their potato laptop. Nothing can describe what you feel the first time you ever install a distribution by yourself. Flashing the image onto that USB, going into that scary curses-like BIOS interface, booting from it, seeing a whole operating system essentially load and run from RAM. Trying out the snappiness of your actions in the user interface, with your eyes widened, you soon realize that this was how computers were meant to be used. You click install and the rest becomes history.

I was 14, and I had discovered cmd.exe.

That was how I started out. My first distro was PopOS. And god did I fall in love with computers by daily driving it. The debian based distro gave me all the goodies that came with a mutable system. The ease of use, the `.deb` packages just working out of the box, the software store giving me whatever I needed. I wasn't a foreigner to the command line, having used it in Windows as a part of my rebellious teen hacker era, but ease of use was always appreciated. 

The familiar pop shell would stay with me through out my whole distrohopping journey, and I went as far as trying my best to get it on other systems as well. And even after trying out new distros, I kept coming back to it until they announced that pop os wasn't going to sport any new gnome versions, instead ship with their own desktop environment.

I was 15, and I had started learning web design.

While still in love with pop, i felt the allure of other linux distros. Garuda's dragonized version was especially attractive to me, so i tried it next. I stuck with it for the next few months, and realised that its flashiness wasn't for me. So I came back to PopOS, but i had grown used to the btrfs file system, and the zen kernel that I had configured. I swapped out my existing kernel with the Liquorix kernel, and experimented with different file systems. I learned about swap and EFI partitions. I didn't use windows back then, having clean installed my distro, but soon realised that it was a necessary evil- my bios updates only came from windows. So I dual booted it, and created a shared partition accessible by both the operating systems. 

This worked for a few months. I tried Garuda's gnome version later, mimicking my same setup, trying out different kernels, learning about their pros and cons, learning about setting up your system. I tried vanilla os with its incredible ability to run packages from any distribution. I tried out Endevour OS with its cool wallpapers. I tried out Manjaro and found it to be a nice distribution regardless of its reputation. I used Kali Linux and Parrot OS because apparently the allure of hacking hadn't died out (little did I know it never would, and I would base my whole future off of it)(TBD). NixOS caught my fancy too, but due to its inability to be fhs compliant, it sadly didnt work for me. Debian was no stranger.

From every distro I have ever used, I learned something new.
From PopOS, it was the way the default `.bashrc` was configured, and dotfiles.
From Garuda, it was the btrfs file system and the zen kernel.
From Vanilla OS, it was how immutable distros worked and how distrobox did most of what I wanted out of it.
From Kali Linux and Parrot OS, all the different security measures one takes to harden their system.
From debian, its simplicity.

I started using git to manage my dotfiles on github. My long dead account saw activity almost every day.

I tried kde. I tried xfce. I tried Cinnamon. I tried COSMIC when it was first released. I kept coming back to GNOME.

I was 16. I knew things about computers that made people working in IT uncomfortable.

Later I realised, this journey lead to a cognitive bias known as Maslow's Hammer. If you have a hammer, everything seems like a nail. If you're overly reliable on a tool, you try to use it all the time at everything. This was especially true, because I started using Termux and Tasker on my Android device. Slowly I started down the rabbithole of custom ROMs. 

I sought out the UNIX philosophy and tried to live using Libre software. I consumed tech related content- dystopian, redpilled. A person who had never had a phone until he was out of highschool, was neck deep in tech. Luke Smith was my lord, bugswriter my saviour. Didn't matter if my life was falling apart in the real world. But on the computer, on the internet, I carved out a place for myself. I started using old reddit. I started hanging out on shady forums. I joined long-dead IRC channels. I connected to BBS systems via telnet. I learned to use the terminal. I ssh'ed into my accounts from my school and college. I still do.

I bought a raspberry pi and lived out all my younger self's dreams on it. I made myself more technical than emotional. I read dystopian books that I felt made me even more red pilled. I consumed tech-history. I tried to build an emulator and failed. I kept up with tech news. That guy who everyone consults regarding their tech decisions? That was me.

I made websites for friends. I scraped websites to make some quick cash.

I found like-wise individuals away from keyboard. They taught me new things. We built bots on discord and raided servers of people who we didn't like. We bridged discord and IRC channels. We cracked test portals with javascript snippets and Tampermonkey scripts. 
I switched to arch. Built the perfect system. Broke it down and reassembled it bit by bit. Kintsugi.

I was 17. The real world was just a place I'd temporarily be in.

I was a pirate. Whatever I wanted to watch or read, I could with a few keystroked. Whatever game I wanted to play, I did. 

It wasn't enough. It never will be enough.
